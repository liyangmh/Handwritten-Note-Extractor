Lithium (Li) metal is considered an ideal anode material for future high-energy rechargeable batteries. However,
the extremely unstable interfaces between lithium anode and electrolyte induce uncontrollable dendrite for­
mation and low Coulombic efficiency, retarding its practical applications in Li metal batteries (LMB). Herein, we
propose a facile and feasible strategy of introducing 12-Crown-4 ether as a leveling additive into the electrolyte
to inherently eliminate the self-amplification behavior of dendrite growth. The Li+–additive complexes with low
electron-accepting ability are preferentially adsorbed on the initial protuberant tips of Li metal surface, which
can effectively regulate the local polarization resistance and improve the current distribution uniformity. Based
on this principle, a self-leveling electrolyte comprising fluoroethylene carbonate (FEC) solvent and 12-Crown-4
additive is developed. Dense and dendrite-free Li deposition, as well as enhanced interfacial stability is achieved
in such electrolyte, rendering a significantly improved Coulombic efficiency (97.24%) in Li||Cu cells and a su­
perior cycling stability in Li||Li cells. Moreover, a Li metal full-cell with high-loading LiFePO4 cathode (12.5 mg
cm− 2) retains 98% capacity after 100 cycles at 0.5 C. This self-leveling electrolyte approach opens up new
perspective on the mechanistic and methodological aspects for designing safer lithium metal batteries.

1. Introduction
Li metal is one of the highest-performing next-generation anodes due
to its lowest electrode potential and high theoretical specific capacity
[1–3]. When paired with a conversion-type cathode such as sulfur or
oxygen [4–7], a high-energy full battery can be obtained. However, the
unstable interface between Li metal and electrolyte gives rises to inho­
mogeneous lithium deposition and uncontrollable dendrite formation,
as well as low Coulombic efficiency and poor cycle performance [8–10].
The fragility and heterogeneity of the solid electrolyte interphase
(SEI) were identified as the root cause of inhomogeneous current density
distribution and Li dendrite growth [11–13]. Typically, during Li plating
a higher deposition rate may occur at parts of the SEI that have a lower
interface impedance due to their smaller thickness or more ionconductive composition [14,15]. Furthermore, induced by locally
enhanced electric field at the tip of the protrusions, Li deposition will
preferentially occur on existing dendrites, and hence leading to a self-

amplification effect of dendrite growth [16].
Tremendous efforts have been devoted to constructing stable SEI
films, including electrolyte engineering [17–23], artificial protective
layers [24–31], solid-state electrolytes [32,33] and separators [34–36].
Among these strategies, electrolyte engineering offers a convenient
route to suppress Li dendrite growth, because it is compatible with the
existing battery manufacturing process. It has been reported that LiFrich SEI with high uniformity and stability can be in-situ formed by
introducing fluorine-containing co-solvents or additives such as LiF
[37,38], fluoroethylene carbonate (FEC) [39–42], lithium difluoro(ox­
alate)borate (LiDFOB) [43,44] and LiPF6 [45,46]. However, the thick­
ness and composition of these in-situ formed SEIs are nonuniform upon
repeated cycling, especially under high current density, thereby causing
an unstable electrode interface. Moreover, once the uneven Li deposition
occurs, the SEI layer will be easily broken by localized stresses, which
aggravates the inhomogeneous current density distribution and ulti­
mately leads to dendritic growth. Therefore, approaches relying only on

Although the development of effective electrolytes for stable SEI
films and Li deposition is still in its infant stage, leveling additives have
been widely used in traditional metal electroplating such as Cu [47,48],
Ni [49,50], Zn [51,52], Mn [53,54] and Ag [55,56] to improve the
quality of coatings. The leveling abilities of additives used in these
electrolyte solutions are closely related to their influences on cathodic
polarization [57]. Commonly, appropriate amounts of stable complex
ions are formed by coordination of the leveling additives to metal spe­
cies, which can be preferentially adsorbed on the protruding regions of
the cathode surface that have thinner diffusion layer and stronger
electrical field. This preferential adsorption of additives increases the
local overpotential and decreases the deposition rate at the tips, leading
to more uniform distribution of electric current. In the case of Li depo­
sition, this leveling mechanism is expected to prevent protection failure
of SEI film and alleviate the self-amplification behavior during dendrite
growth.
Here, we propose a new strategy of using leveling additives to ach­
ieve dendrite-free lithium deposition in LMBs. A self-leveling electrolyte
comprising FEC solvent and 12-Crown-4 additive has been developed,
showing evident leveling effects and enhanced interfacial stability dur­
ing Li deposition. Moreover, the practical use of the electrolyte is veri­
fied by the improved Coulombic efficiency and prolonged cycle lives in
Li||copper (Cu) half-cells, Li||Li symmetric cells and Li||LiFePO4 (LFP)
full cells.

2.1. Material preparation
Li chips (99.9% purity, 250 µm thickness) and Cu foils (99.9% purity,

Company Ltd. Lithium hexafluorophosphate (LiPF6), ethylene carbonate

required amounts of 12-Crown-4 or 18-Crown-6 to the as-prepared FEC/
DMC electrolyte. All materials were kept and used in an argon-filled
glove box with oxygen and water contents below 0.1 ppm.

2.5. Computational details
The first-principles calculations were conducted in Gaussian (G09)
software package [58]. The M06-2X/6-311G(d,p) density functional
method [59] was employed for geometry optimization and electron af­
finity calculations. The SMD implicit solvation model [60] with acetone
parameters (dielectric constant ε = 20.493) was used to account for the
solvent effect in a mixed carbonate solvent electrolyte. The vertical
electron affinity (VEA) is calculated as the energy difference between
the cationic species (Li+–L, L represents EC, DMC, FEC, 18-Crown-6 or
12-Crown-4) and neutral lithium species (Li0–L) with both at the opti­
mized geometry of the cations (E1-E2), while adiabatic electron affinity
(AEA) is calculated as the energy difference between the cationic and
neutral lithium species at their respective optimized geometries (E1-E3).

2.2. Material characterization
The morphologies of Li deposits and cycled Li metal anodes were
characterized by SIGMA scanning electron microscope (SEM, Zeiss,
Germany) operated at 5.0 kV. The depth profiles of surface chemical
composition were identified by Escalab250Xi X-ray photoelectron
spectrometer (XPS, Thermo Fisher Scientific, USA) at various argon ion
sputtering times. For SEI characterization, Li||Cu cells were discharged
for 0.1 h at 1.0 mA cm− 2 and then charged to the cutoff voltage of 1.0 V.
After 10 cycles, deposited Li metal was stripped from the Cu foil, leaving
the SEI layer on the substrate for analysis. All the above samples ob­
tained from disassembled cells were first rinsed by DMC solvent five
times to remove the remaining electrolytes, and finally dried under
vacuum. To avoid the contamination of ambient oxygen and moisture,
the samples were transported from the glovebox to the SEM and XPS
instruments using an Ar protected transfer box.

leading to a higher total resistance at tips than the flat regions, i.e., Rs-t +

faster deposition rate at flat regions (Dtip < Dflat) and the height differ­

dendrite amplification effect during Li deposition.
The leveling effect originates from the more negative reduction po­
tential of the ion–additive complex (Li+–12-Crown-4) compared with
that of ion–solvent complexes. First-principles calculations (Fig. 2) were
performed to elucidate the interaction of Li+ with three typical ester
solvents (EC, DMC and FEC) and two crown ether additives (12-Crown-4
and 18-Crown-6). The optimized geometrical structures (Fig. 2a) suggest
that Li+ tends to bind with the carbonyl oxygen in EC, DMC and FEC. In
comparison, Li+ binds with four cyclic oxygen atoms in 12-Crown-4 or
three cyclic oxygen atoms in 18-Crown-6. The LUMOs of all the com­
plexes are mainly contributed by the s orbitals of the metal atoms,
indicating that the captured electrons are largely located on Li.

optimized geometries and summarized in Fig. 2c and Fig. S3. In com­

is worth noting that at such a high cathode loading, the amount of Li
metal involved in the plating/stripping during each cycle will greatly
increase, leading to more parasitic reactions and faster capacity fading.
The electrochemical stability of various electrolytes was evaluated using
linear sweep voltammetry (Fig. S16) and cyclic voltammetry (Fig. S17).

cell shows a much smaller increase of voltage hysteresis during cycling
as compared to the additive-free electrolyte (Fig. 6b, Fig. S18c and d),
which is attributed to the formation of more stable and less resistive
interface between Li anode and electrolyte.

4. Conclusion
In summary, we have demonstrated that crown ethers could act as
efficient leveling additives for dendrite-free lithium deposition in LMBs.
A current density distribution model based on the region-specific resis­
tance is proposed, and the leveling mechanism is elucidated by the
electron affinity and LUMO energy level calculations. The ion–additive
complexes exhibit lower electron-accepting ability compared to the
ion–solvent complexes and could effectively regulate the local polari­
zation resistance, leading to a transition of Li deposition behavior from
self-amplification to a self-leveling. Furthermore, a self-leveling elec­
trolyte comprising FEC solvent and 12-Crown-4 additive has been
developed based on their combinational effects, where FEC facilitates
the formation of a robust SEI film and 12-Crown-4 additive produces a
leveling effect during Li deposition. Dense Li morphology and dendritefree deposition, as well as enhanced SEI surface chemistry and interfa­
cial stability has been achieved in such electrolyte, which could signif­
icantly alleviate the electrolyte decomposition and electrode
degradation during the Li plating/stripping process. As a consequence, a
high average Coulombic efficiency of 97.24% for Li||Cu cell, as well as
superior cycling stability over 250 h for Li||Li symmetric cell has been
observed. Moreover, in the Li metal full cell assembled with highloading LiFePO4 (12.5 mg cm− 2), a 98% capacity retention along with
alleviated increase of voltage hysteresis has been accomplished after
100 cycles at 0.5C. This work demonstrates an effective and facile
approach to suppress Li dendrite growth, while the self-leveling elec­
trolyte strategy provides a complement to the existing knowledge
framework on designing the next-generation LMBs.
