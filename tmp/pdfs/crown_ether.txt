However, the commercial applications of

their poor cycle life and safety issues caused

develop safe and efficient LMBs.
Up to now, various strategies have been explored to address
these issues such as optimizing electrolyte composition,[10,11]
employing solid-state electrolytes,[12,13] constructing an artificial
SEI,[14,15] designing 3D materials[16–18] and Li-based alloy.[19,20]
Among the above strategies, SEI plays a significant role in
determining the performance of LMBs due to the reason
that the stable SEI can reduce the side reaction and repress
the formation of Li dendrites.[21] In order to obtain stable and
compact SEI, modification of electrolytes with additives has
attracted numerous research interests.[22–25] It is usually considered that the general role of additives is to participate in the
formation of stable SEI film.[26] However, in fact, the dissociation degree of salts, the strength of solvation and the stability
of solvents dictate the nature of the resulting SEI.[27] In addition, some researches proposed a novel view of Li protection
strategy, which showed that the additive played a coordinator
role in electrolyte systems.[28–31] For example, fluoroethylene
carbonate (FEC) as co-solvent could change the composition
of the solvation sheath, which was beneficial to forming uniform SEI and dendrite-free Li anode.[32] However, it is noted
that the performance of Li-Cu half-cell is still not satisfactory.

Lithium (Li) metal battery is considered the most promising next-generation
battery due to its low potential and high theoretical capacity. However, Li dendrite growth causes serious safety problems. Herein, the 15-Crown-5 (15-C-5)
is reported as an electrolyte additive based on solvation shell regulation. The
strong complex effect between Li+ ion and 15-C-5 can reduce the concentration of Li ions on the electrode surface, thus changing the nucleation, and
repressing the growth of Li dendrites in the plating process. Significantly,
the strong coordination of Li+/15-C-5 would be able to make them aggregate around the Li crystal surface, which could form a protective layer and
favor the formation of a smooth and dense solid electrolyte interphase with
high toughness and Li+ ion conductivity. Therefore, the electrolyte system
with 2.0 wt% 15-C-5 achieves excellent electrochemical performance with
170 cycles at 1.0 mA cm−2 with capacity of 0.5 mA h cm−2 in symmetric Li|Li
cells. The obviously enhanced cycle and rate performance are also achieved
in Li|LiNi0.6Co0.2Mn0.2O2 (NCM622) full cells. The 15-C-5 demonstrates to be
a promising additive for the electrolytes toward safe and efficient Li metal
batteries.

2. Results and Discussion
It is generally accepted that the uneven nucleation leads to
the dendrite growth in conventional electrolytes. Meanwhile, the
charges gathered at the tip of dendrite will further promote the
growth of Li dendrites, leading to the rapid damage of Li metal
battery (Figure 1). In contrast, when crown ether is introduced
into electrolyte, the Li+/crown ether complexes can reduce the
surface concentration of Li ions to form large primary crystal
nucleus. At the plating progress, the Li+/crown ether complexes
not only reduce the aggregation of Li ions on the dendrite
surface, but also can isolate carbonate molecules, which will
form uniform and dense SEI films to repress Li dendrites and
enhance the cycling performance of Li battery.
Li plating process with the crown ether additives was
illustrated by scanning electron microscopy (SEM). Li
was plated at a current density of 1.0 mA cm−2 with a fixed
capacity of 0.5 mAh cm−2. Without additives, the large Li
nucleation deposits were observed on the Li foil surface at

5 cycles (Figure 2a,b). After a further 50 cycles, the mossy
and foamy Li deposits covered the whole surface of Li metal
foil (Figure S1a,b, Supporting Information). By contrast, with
the addition of 2.0 wt% 12-Crown-4 (12-C-4) (Figure 2c,d) and
2.0 wt% 15-C-5 (Figure 2e,f), the smooth Li deposition layer
could be observed after 5 cycles plating/stripping, respectively,
showing that the additives helped the homogeneous deposition of Li metal. However, after 50 cycles plating/stripping, the
SEM image with 12-C-4 additive looked rougher (Figure S1c,d,
Supporting Information), indicating that the effect of surface
control of Li plating was poorer than 15-C-5 (Figure S1e,f,
Supporting Information). In addition, with the addition of
18-Crown-6 (18-C-6), the deposit layer was similar to the
blank sample, which illustrated the 18-C-8 had little effect to
suppress Li dendrite growth (Figure 2g,h; Figure S1g,h, Supporting Information).
In order to research the dynamic Li deposition process, in
situ optical microscope observation was carried out with different electrolytes. As shown in Figure 2i, without any additive,
uneven dendritic Li was observed after 2 min at a plating current of 5 mA cm2. Moreover, high dendrite and mossy Li deposition layer formed after plating for 12 min, which would result
in the continuous consume of metallic Li, and further lead to
the drop of the CE. In contrast, a more uniform Li deposition
was achieved with the 15-C-5 contained electrolyte with plating
2 min (Figure 2j). After 12 min, the thickness of Li deposition
layer was much smaller than that of blank electrolyte system.
Meanwhile, the in situ observation of 12-C-4 contained electrolyte
was also found to reduce the growth of Li dendrites (Figure S2a,
Supporting Information). The large morphological change
indicates that the 18-C-6 has almost no effect to preserve Li dendrites (Figure S2b, Supporting Information). The morphology
evolution was consistent with the SEM results.

It is noted that the large dendrites are beneficial to improve the
long-term performance of Li battery. As a fundamental understanding, the SEI between the electrolyte and Li metal is one of
important factors to repress Li dendrite growth. The uniform
and dense SEI layer is more beneficial to repress the growth
of Li dendrite.[35] As shown in Figure 4c, the SEI films are full

the formation of new Li dendrites. By contrast, the smooth and
dense SEI layers were observed in the 15-C-5 contained electrolyte (Figure 4f). Therefore, the Li dendrite growth was effectively repressed.
To determine the composition of different SEI films, X-ray
photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) was carried out. The survey
spectrum (Figure S4, Supporting Information) shows that the
SEI main contains C, O, F, and Li. The compositions of the SEI
layer were further probed by the XPS depth profiling of C 1s,
O 1s and Li 1s spectra after regular Ar sputtered the surface.
Figure S5, Supporting Information, shows the O 1s spectra,
where the peaks at 528.2, 531.5, and 532.9 eV correspond
to the LiO, LiCO3, and ROCO2Li, respectively. As shown
in Figure 5a–d, from initial surface to the Li after 4 min Ar
sputtering, the C 1s spectra of all samples are attributed to
C–C bonding at 284.6 eV, C–O bonding at 286.6 eV, ROCO2−1
at 289.1 eV, and Li2CO32− at 290.2 eV, which shows that the
organic and inorganic components are mixed together in SEI
film. This is the typical SEI structure predicted by Peled et al.[36]

the 15-C-5 contained electrolyte system were obviously lower
than that of blank electrolyte system (Figure S6a, Supporting
Information), which indicates that the 15-C-5 could be tightly
contacted with the surface of SEI film to isolate carbonates.

3. Conclusions
In summary, we have demonstrated that crown ethers could
act as efficient electrolyte additives for LMBs. The most effective 15-C-5 showed excellent performance with 170 cycles at
1.0 mA cm−2 with a capacity of 0.5 mA h cm−2 of symmetric
Li|Li cells. The 15-C-5 additive also exhibited the excellent effect
at high current density with 2.0 mA cm−2. In addition, obviously enhanced cycle and rate performance were achieved in
Li|NCM622 full cells. The detailed characterization and theoretical calculations indicate that 15-C-5 can make crystal nucleus
smoothly growth and isolate carbonate electrolyte, participate
and assist to form smooth and dense SEI films, which effectively to repress Li dendrite growth and to boost the long-term
cycle performance of Li metal battery. This work provides an
effective direction to study efficient electrolyte additives for
next-generation LMBs.

4. Experimental Section
Characterization: The morphological change of the lithium foil
was characterized using scanning electron microscopy (SEM,
HitachiS4800). In situ optical microscope was characterized by three-eye
stereomicroscope (Zoom 650, Shanghai Tuming Optical Instrument
Co., Ltd). Cryo-transmission electron microscope (cryo-TEM) were
carried out via a Thermo Fisher Scientific Tecnai Arctica equipped with
a Falcon II direct electron detector camera at a voltage of 200 kV. The
assembly of the optical cell was operated in an argon-filled glovebox.
X-ray photoelectron spectra (XPS) were collected with an in situ XPS
(MDTC-EQ-M20-01), the XPS depth profiles were obtained by preset
different sputtering time. 1H NMR spectrum was carried out by a
nuclear magnetic resonance spectrometer (NMR, Bruker 400 MHz). The
standard DMC solution with 1.0 wt%, 2.0 wt%, 5.0 wt%, and 10.0 wt%
15-C-5 was checked and used as the reference curve.
Electrochemical Measurements: Electrochemical measurements were
evaluated in a 2025-type symmetric coin cells that consisted of the
same Li foil at both sides. The electrolyte was lithium hexafluorophosphate
(LiPF6, 1.0 m) dissolved in a mixture of ethyl carbonate (EC) and dimethyl
carbonate (DMC) in a volume ratio of 1:1 with or without crown additives.
The amounts of crown additives in electrolyte was 1.0 wt%, 2.0 wt%,
and 4.0 wt%, respectively. For cell testing, 60 µL of electrolyte was used
for plating/stripping galvanostatic testing at current densities of 1.0 and
2.0 mA cm−2, respectively. For the full cells test, the NCM622 electrodes
were prepared by mixing NCM622, carbon black and polyvinylidene
fluoride (PVDF) in the ratio of 8:1:1 with N-methyl-2-pyrrolidone (NMP)
as the solvent. The mass loading of the NCM622 electrodes was about
3.4 mg cm−2. The Li foil is used as counter electrode. Galvanostatic
measurements were performance on Neware battery testing system
(CT-4008). Electrochemical impedance spectra (EIS) was carried out in
8-channel electrochemical workstation (IVIUM) with a frequency range
between 0.1 and 100 KHz.

VASP.[41,42] The generalized gradient approximation (GGA) with Perdew–
Burke–Ernzerhof (PBE)[43] exchange-correlation functional was employed
to optimize the structures. The cutoff energy was set to 520 eV and the
convergence criterion for the energy and force was set to 10−6 eV and
